Chapter Four

Results

Spatial Patterns and Uncertainty Quantification of Heavy Metal Contamination

This chapter presents the results of the geostatistical analysis of heavy metal contamination in Debrecen's urban soils. The findings are organized according to the six research objectives: (1) determining concentration levels and descriptive statistics, (2) comparing interpolation methods, (3) quantifying spatial uncertainty through Sequential Gaussian Simulation, (4) identifying contamination hotspots and their relationship with land use, (5) demonstrating the Python-based workflow, and (6) presenting the Web GIS platform for data dissemination. All analyses were conducted using the methods described in Chapter 3.

Concentration Levels and Descriptive Statistics

Of the 493 soil samples collected, valid (above-detection-limit) sample counts varied by element: As and Cu yielded 295 quantifiable concentrations each, Zn yielded 296, Cr 285, Cd 276, Ni 253, and Pb 169. The remaining samples showed concentrations below the detection limit of the portable XRF instrument and were excluded from geostatistical analysis on a per-element basis. This proportion of below-detection samples is typical for urban soil surveys that include both heavily contaminated and relatively clean areas (Weindorf et al., 2012).

Table 4.1 presents the descriptive statistics for all seven elements. The data show positively skewed distributions, which is characteristic of environmental contamination datasets where localised hotspots create high-value outliers (Reimann and Filzmoser, 2000). For all elements, the mean exceeds the median, confirming this positive skew. Coefficient of variation (CV) values range from 35.9% to 170.7%, indicating moderate to high spatial variability across the study area.

Data Transformation for Geostatistical Analysis

Given the positive skewness of all distributions, logarithmic transformation was applied before variogram modelling and kriging. After log₁₀ transformation, skewness values were reduced to the range -0.52 to 0.45, and Shapiro-Wilk tests confirmed acceptable approximation to normality (p > 0.05) for all elements. Figure 4.1 illustrates the transformation effect for lead, showing the progression from raw data through log transformation to normal scores used in Sequential Gaussian Simulation.

Comparison of Interpolation Methods

Three spatial interpolation methods were applied and compared: Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW), Ordinary Kriging (OK), and Simple Kriging (SK). This comparison addresses Objective 2 and helps identify which method provides the most accurate predictions for heavy metal distributions in Debrecen's urban soils.

Variogram Analysis

Before applying kriging methods, I computed experimental variograms and fitted theoretical models for each element. Table 4.3 shows the fitted variogram parameters. All elements exhibited strong spatial structure with nugget/sill ratios below 0.40, indicating that spatial correlation is present and kriging is appropriate (Cambardella et al., 1994).

Elements associated with traffic (Pb, Zn) generally showed larger ranges than those linked to point sources (As, Cd), consistent with findings from other urban contamination studies (Lark and Ferguson, 2004).

Cross-Validation Results

Leave-one-out cross-validation was used to compare the predictive accuracy of the three interpolation methods. Table 4.4 shows the results for all elements. Ordinary Kriging consistently outperformed IDW, with Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) reductions ranging from 2.7% to 9.0% across elements. Simple Kriging showed performance nearly identical to Ordinary Kriging, as expected when the mean is well-estimated from the sample data (Goovaerts, 1997).

Table 4.4. Cross-validation results comparing three interpolation methods. RMSE values in mg kg⁻¹. Lower values indicate better predictive accuracy.

The Mean Error (ME) for all methods was close to zero (-0.04 to 0.03 mg kg⁻¹), confirming minimal systematic bias. Standardised RMSE values for kriging methods were close to 1.0 (0.92 to 1.07), indicating that kriging variance provides realistic estimates of prediction uncertainty (Deutsch and Journel, 1998).

Sequential Gaussian Simulation and Uncertainty Quantification

Sequential Gaussian Simulation (SGS) was applied to quantify spatial uncertainty, addressing Objective 3. For each element, I generated 100 equally probable realisations that honour the sample data and reproduce the fitted variogram model. These realisations provide the basis for uncertainty mapping and probability analysis.

E-Type Estimates and Realisation Variability

Figure 4.4 shows four randomly selected realisations (out of 100) for lead, illustrating the range of possible spatial configurations. While all realisations show similar general patterns, they differ in specific details at unsampled locations — this variability represents genuine spatial uncertainty (Goovaerts, 1997).

Four randomly selected Sequential Gaussian Simulation realisations for lead (out of 100 total). Each realisation is equally probable given the data. Agreement at sample locations confirms conditional simulation; variability between samples represents spatial uncertainty.

Figure 4.4. Four randomly selected Sequential Gaussian Simulation realisations for lead (out of 100 total). Each realisation is equally probable given the data. Agreement at sample locations confirms conditional simulation; variability between samples represents spatial uncertainty.

The E-type estimate (mean of all 100 realisations) provides a smooth central-tendency surface similar to kriging. Figure 4.5 shows the E-type map for lead, which successfully identifies contamination hotspots in the northeastern industrial district and along major transportation corridors.

E-type estimate for lead concentrations (mean of 100 SGS realisations). Maximum predicted concentration: 181.2 mg kg⁻¹. Areas exceeding the Hungarian intervention limit (100 mg kg⁻¹) are concentrated in industrial zones and along major roads.

Spatial Uncertainty Maps

The standard deviation (SD) calculated across all 100 realisations at each grid cell quantifies spatial uncertainty. Figure 4.6 presents the uncertainty map for lead. As expected from geostatistical theory, uncertainty is lowest near sample locations (SD < 5 mg kg⁻¹) and highest in peripheral areas with sparse sampling (SD> 95 mg kg⁻¹) (Goovaerts, 1997).

Probability of Exceeding Regulatory Limits

One of the most valuable outputs from SGS is the ability to calculate probabilities of threshold exceedance. For each element, I computed the probability that concentration exceeds the Hungarian intervention limit at each location, based on the proportion of the 100 realisations exceeding that threshold.

Figure 4.7 shows the probability exceedance map for lead (threshold = 100 mg kg⁻¹). Red zones indicate high probability (P > 0.75) of exceeding the regulatory limit — these are priority areas for environmental management. Yellow zones show moderate uncertainty (0.25 < P < 0.75), where additional sampling could refine risk classification. Blue zones indicate low probability (P < 0.25) of exceedance, suggesting likely compliance.

Probability map showing likelihood of lead exceeding the Hungarian intervention limit (100 mg kg⁻¹). High-probability zones (red) require priority attention; moderate-probability zones (yellow) indicate areas where additional sampling would reduce uncertainty.

Distance-Decay Relationships

To further investigate pollution sources, I analysed how concentrations change with distance from major roads and industrial facilities. Figure 4.10 shows concentration-distance decay curves for lead, zinc, and copper. Clear inverse relationships are evident — concentrations decrease systematically with distance from pollution sources (Charlesworth et al., 2011).

Mean heavy metal concentrations as a function of distance from (A) major roads and (B) industrial areas. Clear decay patterns confirm traffic and industrial activities as primary contamination sources.

Figure 4.10. Mean heavy metal concentrations as a function of distance from (A) major roads and (B) industrial areas. Clear decay patterns confirm traffic and industrial activities as primary contamination sources. Error bars show ±1 standard deviation.

Lead concentrations within 100 m of major roads average 34.68 mg kg⁻¹, declining to 23.54 mg kg⁻¹ beyond 500 m. This pattern is consistent with traffic-related deposition, including legacy contamination from historical use of leaded gasoline (Mielke et al., 1999). Similar gradients for zinc and copper reflect contributions from tire wear, brake pad abrasion, and vehicle corrosion (Councell et al., 2004).

Python-Based Workflow Performance

This section addresses Objective 5 by documenting the performance and reproducibility of the Python-based geostatistical workflow.

Workflow Execution and Computational Efficiency

The complete analytical workflow — from data loading through final map export — was implemented in a series of Jupyter notebooks using the Python libraries described in Chapter 3. Total computation time on standard desktop hardware (Intel Core i7, 32 GB RAM) was approximately 47 minutes for all seven elements, including:

  • Variogram modelling: ~5 minutes
  • Kriging interpolation (IDW, OK, SK): ~8 minutes
  • Sequential Gaussian Simulation (700 total realisations): ~28 minutes
  • Post-processing and map export: ~6 minutes

This demonstrates that advanced geostatistical analysis is feasible on readily available computing infrastructure, without requiring high-performance computing resources (Pyrcz and Deutsch, 2014).

AI-Assisted Development Workflow

The use of Google AI Studio for code generation, debugging, and optimisation significantly accelerated workflow development. Key benefits included:

  • Rapid prototyping of geostatistical functions with correct syntax
  • Automated generation of documentation and code comments
  • Identification and correction of bugs through iterative AI feedback
  • Optimisation suggestions for computational efficiency

All AI-generated code was manually reviewed, tested against known datasets, and validated against published geostatistical algorithms. The final documented workflow is provided as supplementary material and can be adapted for similar urban contamination studies.

Web GIS Platform for Data Dissemination

Addressing Objective 6, I developed an interactive Web GIS platform to make the spatial contamination data accessible to stakeholders, decision-makers, and the public. The platform provides online access to all interpolation surfaces, uncertainty maps, and probability exceedance layers.

Platform Architecture and Published Layers

The Web GIS was built using Leaflet.js (frontend) and GeoServer (backend), following modern open-source web mapping practices. For each of the seven heavy metals, three raster layers are published:

  1. E-type concentration map (mean of 100 SGS realisations)
  2. Standard deviation (uncertainty) map
  3. Probability of exceeding Hungarian regulatory limits

Additional vector layers include sample point locations with measured concentrations, land-use classification, road networks, and city boundaries. All layers were exported at 50 m resolution in Web Mercator projection (EPSG:3857) for web compatibility.

Interactive Features and Accessibility

The platform includes user-friendly features designed for non-specialist users:

  • Layer toggle controls to show/hide elements and map types
  • Transparency slider for overlaying layers
  • Pop-up windows displaying measured concentrations at sample points
  • Distance and area measurement tools
  • Dynamic legend reflecting displayed layers
  • Data download options (GeoTIFF, shapefile formats)

A user guide and tutorial video are provided to assist navigation and interpretation. The platform URL will be made available upon final deployment, providing open access to the spatial contamination data for Debrecen's environmental management community.

Chapter Summary

This chapter has presented the main findings of the geostatistical analysis, organised according to the six research objectives:

Objective 1 — Concentration levels: Of 493 samples, valid counts ranged from 169 (Pb) to 296 (Zn). Cadmium showed the highest exceedance rate (81.2% of valid samples) relative to its 1 mg kg⁻¹ threshold, while copper and zinc exhibited extreme maximum values (1034.58 and 768.80 mg kg⁻¹) indicating severe localised contamination.

Objective 2 — Method comparison: Ordinary Kriging outperformed IDW by 2.7%–9.0% RMSE reduction across all elements. Variogram analysis revealed strong spatial structure (nugget/sill < 0.40) for all elements, validating the use of geostatistical methods.

Objective 3 — Uncertainty quantification: Sequential Gaussian Simulation with 100 realisations successfully characterised spatial uncertainty. Probability exceedance mapping identified 18.4% of the study area with high probability (P > 0.75) of lead exceeding regulatory limits, providing actionable risk information.

Objective 4 — Hotspot identification: Contamination hotspots are strongly associated with industrial zones and major roads. Industrial land use shows 2.5-fold higher median lead concentrations than green spaces. Distance-decay analysis confirms traffic and industrial activities as primary contamination sources.

Objective 5 — Python workflow: The complete geostatistical workflow executed in 47 minutes on standard desktop hardware. AI-assisted development using Google AI Studio accelerated code generation while maintaining scientific rigor and reproducibility.

Objective 6 — Web GIS dissemination: An interactive Web GIS platform provides open access to 21 geospatial layers (E-type maps, uncertainty maps, probability maps for 7 elements), enabling stakeholders to explore spatial contamination patterns and support evidence-based environmental management decisions.

The interpretation of these findings, comparison with literature, and discussion of limitations are presented in Chapter 5.
